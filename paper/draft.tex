\title{Combinatorics of Saddle Points in Deep Linear Networks}
\author{}
\date{}

\begin{abstract}
We study the rank-\(r\) critical loci of deep linear networks directly in weight
space. Fixing a singular subset \(S\) of the teacher matrix determines a closing
map
\[
W_0=\Sigma_{XY}P_Q,
\]
and the transpose-gradient equations define a cyclic locus
\(\Gamma_{d,S}\subseteq \Omega\). Its rank-\(r\) critical locus
\(\mathcal C_{d,S}^r\) is the distinguished fiber cut out by the additional
constraint
\[
\mu(W)=P_SW^\ast.
\]
Our first result is a direct weight-space orbit correspondence:
\(\mathcal C_{d,S}^r/H_S\) is in bijection with \(\Gamma_{d,S}^r/G_d^{W_0}\).

We then identify \(\Gamma_{d,S}\) with a fixed-arrow representation variety of
the weight-space cyclic Nakayama algebra \(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\) and classify its
indecomposable modules. This yields an orbit stratification of the cyclic locus
by multiplicity patterns. To obtain the combinatorics relevant for saddle
points, we introduce lifted cyclic rank patterns on the universal cover of the
cycle and prove that multiplicity patterns and rank patterns are equivalent via
explicit forward and inversion formulas. As a consequence, the cyclic locus and
the critical locus are both stratified by admissible cyclic rank patterns.

Finally, because every stratum is a single group orbit, we derive exact
codimension formulas from orbit dimensions. The codimension of the critical
locus is obtained by minimizing an explicit quadratic functional over the
admissible cyclic rank patterns, and it differs from the codimension of the
rank-\(r\) cyclic locus by the universal endpoint term
\[
r(d_0-d_L+r).
\]
In the full-rank case \(r=d_L\), our results recover the global-minimum fiber
geometry studied by Pepin Lehalleur and Rim\'anyi.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Critical points of deep linear networks are highly structured and highly
degenerate. Unlike Morse-type landscapes, the relevant objects are not isolated
points but algebraic loci carrying large symmetry groups. For the purpose of
understanding saddle points, one therefore needs a discrete combinatorial label
for an entire critical stratum rather than a local description of a single
point.

Two recent developments motivate the present work. Achour, Malgouyres, and
Gerchinovitz gave a complete second-order classification of critical points of
deep linear networks and showed that the vanishing-gradient equations can be
rewritten as cyclic vanishing relations \cite{achour2024loss}. On the other
hand, Pepin Lehalleur and Rim\'anyi studied the fiber of the multiplication map
at a fixed end-to-end matrix and gave a quiver-theoretic description of the
geometry of global minima \cite{lehalleur2024geometry}. The aim of the present
paper is to connect these two viewpoints and obtain a combinatorial description
of saddle strata of fixed rank.

The main structural choice in this draft is to work directly in the parameter
space
\[
\Omega=\prod_{\ell=1}^L \Mat_{d_\ell\times d_{\ell-1}}(\R)
\]
of the network. Previous versions of the project encoded the cyclic equations in
a residual parameter space and then lifted the resulting data back to weight
space. Here we keep both the cyclic locus and the critical locus inside
\(\Omega\) from the start and express their relation by an orbit correspondence.
This direct weight-space formulation is better suited to saddle combinatorics,
because the labels that classify the cyclic locus transfer immediately to the
critical locus.

\subsection*{Contributions}

The paper makes four contributions.

\begin{enumerate}
\item We prove a direct weight-space orbit correspondence between the cyclic
  locus and the critical locus:
  \[
  \mathcal C_{d,S}^r/H_S \xrightarrow{\sim} \Gamma_{d,S}^r/G_d^{W_0}.
  \]

\item We identify the fixed-arrow cyclic locus with a representation variety of a
  cyclic monomial Nakayama algebra \(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\), classify its
  indecomposable modules, and recover the orbit stratification by multiplicity
  patterns.

\item We introduce lifted cyclic rank patterns on the universal cover of the
  cycle, prove an explicit bijection between multiplicity patterns and rank
  patterns, and deduce a stratification of both \(\Gamma_{d,S}^r\) and
  \(\mathcal C_{d,S}^r\) by admissible cyclic rank patterns.

\item We derive exact codimension formulas for the cyclic and critical strata by
  orbit-dimension arguments. The codimension of the critical locus is obtained
  by minimizing an explicit quadratic functional on the admissible rank-pattern
  set.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Related work}

The starting point for the critical-point analysis is the work of Achour,
Malgouyres, and Gerchinovitz \cite{achour2024loss}, who classify critical
points of deep linear networks at second order and show how the singular-value
decomposition of the teacher matrix controls the critical values. Our use of the
cyclic equations follows this line.

The global-minimum benchmark comes from Pepin Lehalleur and Rim\'anyi
\cite{lehalleur2024geometry}, who study fibers of the multiplication map by
quiver-representation methods. Their work provides the correct full-rank model
for the present paper: when \(r=d_L\), the closing map vanishes, the cyclic
locus becomes the full-rank determinantal locus, and our saddle-stratification
results specialize to the orbit geometry of the multiplication-map fiber.

On the representation-theoretic side, we use only standard facts on bound
quivers, Nakayama algebras, and Krull--Schmidt decompositions; a general
reference is \cite{assem2006}.

\subsection*{Organization}

Section~2 introduces the direct weight-space setup. Section~3 proves the orbit
correspondence between the cyclic and critical loci. Section~4 identifies the
cyclic locus with a fixed-arrow representation variety and classifies its orbits
by multiplicity patterns. Section~5 replaces multiplicity labels by cyclic rank
patterns. Section~6 transfers the rank-pattern stratification to the critical
locus, giving the main theorem of the paper. Section~7 gives exact codimension
formulas. The appendices collect a detailed inversion proof and the homological
comparison with the older \(\Ext^1\)-based viewpoint.

Whenever a proposition has been checked in Lean in the companion formalization,
we state this explicitly after the proof.

\subsection{Deep linear networks}

\begin{definition}[Deep linear network and product map]
Let
\[
\Omega:=\prod_{\ell=1}^L \Mat_{d_\ell\times d_{\ell-1}}(\R),
\qquad
W=(W_1,\dots,W_L)\in \Omega.
\]
The associated deep linear network is
\[
x\longmapsto W_L\cdots W_1x,
\]
and its end-to-end matrix is given by the product map
\[
\mu:\Omega\to \Mat_{d_L\times d_0}(\R),
\qquad
\mu(W):=W_L\cdots W_1.
\]
\end{definition}

For each \(1\leq \ell\leq L\), we use the partial-product notation
\[
W_{>\ell}:=W_L\cdots W_{\ell+1}\in \Mat_{d_L\times d_\ell}(\R),
\qquad
W_{<\ell}:=W_{\ell-1}\cdots W_1\in \Mat_{d_{\ell-1}\times d_0}(\R),
\]
with the conventions
\[
W_{>L}=I_{d_L},
\qquad
W_{<1}=I_{d_0}.
\]

\begin{definition}[Population loss and teacher matrix]
Let
\[
\mathcal L(W):=\mathbb E\bigl[\|Y-\mu(W)X\|_2^2\bigr].
\]
Write
\[
\Sigma_X=\mathbb E[XX^\top],
\qquad
\Sigma_{XY}=\mathbb E[XY^\top],
\qquad
\Sigma_{YX}=\Sigma_{XY}^\top.
\]
The teacher matrix is
\[
W^\ast:=\Sigma_{YX}\Sigma_X^{-1},
\qquad
r_{\max}:=\rk(W^\ast).
\]
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Standing assumptions.}
Throughout the paper we assume that \(\Sigma_X\) is invertible, that
\(\Sigma_{XY}\) has full rank \(d_L\), that \(W^\ast\) has distinct nonzero
singular values, and that the network has no bottleneck:
\[
d_\ell\geq d_L
\qquad
\text{for every }0\leq \ell\leq L.
\]

\subsection{Gradient equations in weight space}

\begin{proposition}[Gradient formula]
For each \(1\leq \ell\leq L\),
\[
\nabla_{W_\ell}\mathcal L(W)
=
2\,W_{>\ell}^{\top}\bigl(\mu(W)\Sigma_X-\Sigma_{YX}\bigr)W_{<\ell}^{\top}.
\]
Consequently,
\[
\bigl(\nabla_{W_\ell}\mathcal L(W)\bigr)^\top
=
2\,W_{<\ell}\bigl(\Sigma_X\mu(W)^\top-\Sigma_{XY}\bigr)W_{>\ell}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The differential of the product map in the direction \(\dot W_\ell\) is
\[
D\mu(W)[\dot W_\ell]=W_{>\ell}\dot W_\ell W_{<\ell}.
\]
The gradient of the quadratic loss with respect to the end-to-end matrix \(M\)
is
\[
\nabla_M\mathcal L(M)=2(M\Sigma_X-\Sigma_{YX}),
\]
so the chain rule gives the first identity. The second follows by taking the
transpose.
\end{proof}

Fix a singular subset \(S\subseteq \{1,\dots,r_{\max}\}\) of cardinality
\(|S|=r\), and let
\[
P_S,\ P_Q
\]
be the corresponding complementary orthogonal projectors on \(\R^{d_L}\).
Define
\[
W_0:=\Sigma_{XY}P_Q.
\]

\begin{proposition}[Weight-space cyclic equations]
Let \(W\) be a critical point of rank \(r\) attached to \(S\), so that
\[
\mu(W)=P_SW^\ast.
\]
Then the critical-point equations are equivalent to
\[
W_{<\ell}W_0W_{>\ell}=0
\qquad
\text{for every }1\leq \ell\leq L.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
At a critical point attached to \(S\),
\[
\mu(W)=P_SW^\ast=P_S\Sigma_{YX}\Sigma_X^{-1}.
\]
Hence
\[
\Sigma_X\mu(W)^\top-\Sigma_{XY}
=
\Sigma_X\Sigma_X^{-1}\Sigma_{XY}P_S-\Sigma_{XY}
=
\Sigma_{XY}(P_S-I)
=
-\Sigma_{XY}P_Q
=
-W_0.
\]
Substituting this identity into the transpose-gradient formula yields the
claimed equations.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Determinantal, cyclic, and critical loci]
Define
\[
\Sigma_d^r:=\{W\in \Omega:\rk(\mu(W))=r\},
\]
\[
\Gamma_{d,S}
:=
\left\{
W\in \Omega
\ \middle|\
W_{<\ell}W_0W_{>\ell}=0
\text{ for all }1\leq \ell\leq L
\right\},
\]
\[
\Gamma_{d,S}^r:=\Gamma_{d,S}\cap \Sigma_d^r,
\]
and
\[
\mathcal C_{d,S}^r
:=
\left\{
W\in \Gamma_{d,S}^r
\ \middle|\
\mu(W)=P_SW^\ast
\right\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\section{From the Cyclic Locus to the Critical Locus}

\subsection{Symmetry groups}

\begin{definition}[Relevant symmetry groups]
The full base-change group is
\[
G_d:=\prod_{\ell=0}^L \GL(d_\ell,\R),
\]
acting on \(\Omega\) by
\[
(g\cdot W)_\ell:=g_\ell W_\ell g_{\ell-1}^{-1}.
\]
The stabilizer of the closing map is
\[
G_d^{W_0}
:=
\{g=(g_0,\dots,g_L)\in G_d\mid g_0W_0g_L^{-1}=W_0\}.
\]
Finally, the stabilizer of the distinguished critical product is
\[
H_S
:=
\Stab_{G_d^{W_0}}(P_SW^\ast)
=
\{g\in G_d^{W_0}\mid g_L(P_SW^\ast)g_0^{-1}=P_SW^\ast\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Equivariance of the product map]
For every \(g\in G_d\) and every \(W\in \Omega\),
\[
\mu(g\cdot W)=g_L\mu(W)g_0^{-1}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Expand the product and cancel adjacent inverse factors:
\[
\mu(g\cdot W)
=(g_LW_Lg_{L-1}^{-1})\cdots (g_1W_1g_0^{-1})
=g_LW_L\cdots W_1g_0^{-1}
=g_L\mu(W)g_0^{-1}.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Stability of the cyclic locus]
The loci \(\Gamma_{d,S}\) and \(\Gamma_{d,S}^r\) are stable under
\(G_d^{W_0}\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For fixed \(\ell\), set
\[
E_\ell(W):=W_{<\ell}W_0W_{>\ell}.
\]
If \(g\in G_d^{W_0}\), then
\[
(g\cdot W)_{<\ell}=g_{\ell-1}W_{<\ell}g_0^{-1},
\qquad
(g\cdot W)_{>\ell}=g_LW_{>\ell}g_\ell^{-1},
\]
so
\[
E_\ell(g\cdot W)
=
g_{\ell-1}W_{<\ell}(g_0^{-1}W_0g_L)W_{>\ell}g_\ell^{-1}
=
g_{\ell-1}E_\ell(W)g_\ell^{-1}.
\]
Thus the cyclic equations are preserved, and the rank of \(\mu(W)\) is preserved
by equivariance of \(\mu\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[The critical locus is not \(G_d^{W_0}\)-stable for \(r>0\)]
Assume \(r>0\). Then \(\mathcal C_{d,S}^r\) is not stable under
\(G_d^{W_0}\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Choose \(\lambda\in \R^\times\) with \(\lambda\neq 1\), and define
\[
g_0=I_{d_0},
\qquad
g_L=\lambda P_S+P_Q,
\qquad
g_\ell=I_{d_\ell}\quad (1\leq \ell\leq L-1).
\]
Then
\[
g_0W_0g_L^{-1}
=
\Sigma_{XY}P_Q(\lambda^{-1}P_S+P_Q)
=
\Sigma_{XY}P_Q
=
W_0,
\]
so \(g\in G_d^{W_0}\). If \(W\in \mathcal C_{d,S}^r\), then
\[
\mu(g\cdot W)=g_L(P_SW^\ast)=\lambda P_SW^\ast\neq P_SW^\ast.
\]
Hence \(g\cdot W\notin \mathcal C_{d,S}^r\).
\end{proof}

\subsection{Endpoint reduction and normalization}

\begin{definition}[Endpoint group]
Define
\[
K_S
:=
\{(g_0,g_L)\in \GL(d_0,\R)\times \GL(d_L,\R)\mid g_0W_0g_L^{-1}=W_0\},
\]
acting on \(\Mat_{d_L\times d_0}(\R)\) by
\[
(g_0,g_L)\cdot M:=g_LMg_0^{-1}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Endpoint projection]
The projection
\[
\pi:G_d^{W_0}\to K_S,
\qquad
\pi(g_0,\dots,g_L):=(g_0,g_L),
\]
is surjective. Moreover, for every \(W\in \Omega\),
\[
\mu(G_d^{W_0}\cdot W)=K_S\cdot \mu(W).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If \((g_0,g_L)\in K_S\), then
\[
(g_0,I_{d_1},\dots,I_{d_{L-1}},g_L)\in G_d^{W_0},
\]
so \(\pi\) is surjective. The orbit identity follows from equivariance of
\(\mu\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Endpoint reduction]
For \(W\in \Gamma_{d,S}^r\), the following are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}
\item there exists \(g\in G_d^{W_0}\) such that \(g\cdot W\in \mathcal C_{d,S}^r\);
\item there exists \((g_0,g_L)\in K_S\) such that
  \[
  g_L\mu(W)g_0^{-1}=P_SW^\ast;
  \]
\item \(\mu(W)\in K_S\cdot(P_SW^\ast)\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
This is immediate from the endpoint projection lemma and the definition of the
critical locus.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Visible product-level constraints]
If \(W\in \Gamma_{d,S}\) and \(M=\mu(W)\), then
\[
W_0M=0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
MW_0=0.
\]
Consequently,
\[
\im(M)\subseteq \Ker(W_0),
\qquad
\im(W_0)\subseteq \Ker(M).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For \(\ell=1\), the cyclic equation reads
\[
W_0W_{>1}=0.
\]
Multiplying on the right by \(W_1\) gives \(W_0M=0\). For \(\ell=L\), the cyclic
equation reads \(W_{<L}W_0=0\). Multiplying on the left by \(W_L\) gives
\(MW_0=0\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Rank-\(r\) sharpening]
Assume \(\Sigma_{XY}\) has full rank \(d_L\). If \(W\in \Gamma_{d,S}^r\) and
\(M=\mu(W)\), then
\[
\Ker(W_0)=\im(P_S),
\qquad
\rk(W_0)=d_L-r,
\qquad
\im(M)=\im(P_S).
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Because \(\Sigma_{XY}\) has full rank \(d_L\), it is injective as a map from
\(\R^{d_L}\) to \(\R^{d_0}\). Hence
\[
\Ker(W_0)=\Ker(\Sigma_{XY}P_Q)=\Ker(P_Q)=\im(P_S).
\]
Since \(M\) has rank \(r\) and \(\im(M)\subseteq \Ker(W_0)\), the inclusion is
an equality.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Normalization theorem]
\label{thm:normalization}
Assume that \(\Sigma_{XY}\) has full rank \(d_L\). Then for every
\[
W\in \Gamma_{d,S}^r
\]
one has
\[
\mu(W)\in K_S\cdot (P_SW^\ast).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Set
\[
\sigma:=W_0,
\qquad
T:=P_SW^\ast,
\qquad
U:=\Ker(\sigma)\subseteq \R^{d_L},
\qquad
I:=\im(\sigma)\subseteq \R^{d_0}.
\]
By the previous proposition and corollary, if \(M=\mu(W)\) with
\(W\in \Gamma_{d,S}^r\), then
\[
\sigma M=0,
\qquad
M\sigma=0,
\qquad
\im(M)=U.
\]
Therefore \(M\) induces a surjective map
\[
\overline M:\R^{d_0}/I\to U.
\]

The distinguished matrix \(T=P_SW^\ast\) satisfies the same two annihilation
relations and has image \(U\). Indeed, \(W_0T=0\) follows from \(P_QP_S=0\), and
\(TW_0=0\) is part of the rank-\(r\) critical-point description of
\cite{achour2024loss}; hence \(T\) induces a surjective map
\[
\overline T:\R^{d_0}/I\to U.
\]

Now every element of \(K_S\) preserves \(U\) and \(I\). Conversely, by choosing
complements of \(U\) and \(I\), one checks that the induced action of \(K_S\) on
reduced maps is the natural left-right action of
\[
\GL(U)\times \GL(\R^{d_0}/I),
\]
and that this action is surjective. Since \(\overline M\) and \(\overline T\)
are both surjective maps from \(\R^{d_0}/I\) onto \(U\), they lie in the same
\(\GL(U)\times \GL(\R^{d_0}/I)\)-orbit. Lifting back to \(K_S\) yields
\[
M\in K_S\cdot T.
\]
\end{proof}

\subsection{Orbit correspondence}

\begin{theorem}[Orbit-surjectivity]
Every \(G_d^{W_0}\)-orbit in \(\Gamma_{d,S}^r\) meets the critical locus
\(\mathcal C_{d,S}^r\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(W\in \Gamma_{d,S}^r\). By Theorem~\ref{thm:normalization},
\[
\mu(W)\in K_S\cdot (P_SW^\ast).
\]
Endpoint reduction therefore gives \(g\in G_d^{W_0}\) such that
\[
g\cdot W\in \mathcal C_{d,S}^r.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Direct weight-space orbit correspondence]
\label{thm:orbit-bijection}
Every \(G_d^{W_0}\)-orbit in \(\Gamma_{d,S}^r\) meets \(\mathcal C_{d,S}^r\) in
exactly one \(H_S\)-orbit. Equivalently, the inclusion
\[
\mathcal C_{d,S}^r\hookrightarrow \Gamma_{d,S}^r
\]
induces a bijection
\[
\mathcal C_{d,S}^r/H_S
\xrightarrow{\sim}
\Gamma_{d,S}^r/G_d^{W_0}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Existence is orbit-surjectivity. For uniqueness, let \(W,W'\in \mathcal C_{d,S}^r\)
lie in the same \(G_d^{W_0}\)-orbit, say \(W'=g\cdot W\). Since both points lie
in the critical locus,
\[
\mu(W)=P_SW^\ast=\mu(W').
\]
By equivariance of \(\mu\),
\[
P_SW^\ast
=
g_L(P_SW^\ast)g_0^{-1},
\]
so \(g\in H_S\). Thus
\[
\bigl(G_d^{W_0}\cdot W\bigr)\cap \mathcal C_{d,S}^r=H_S\cdot W.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Transfer of stratifications]
\label{cor:transfer-strata}
Any decomposition of \(\Gamma_{d,S}^r\) into locally closed
\(G_d^{W_0}\)-stable unions of orbits transfers, by intersection with
\(\mathcal C_{d,S}^r\), to a decomposition of \(\mathcal C_{d,S}^r\) into
locally closed \(H_S\)-stable unions of orbits.
\end{corollary}

\section{The Weight-Space Cyclic Algebra and Multiplicity Patterns}

\subsection{Bound-quiver description}

\begin{definition}[The cyclic quiver and the weight-space ideal]
Let
\[
Q=C_{L+1}
\]
be the oriented cycle on vertices
\[
Q_0=\{0,1,\dots,L\}
\]
with arrows
\[
a_t:t-1\to t
\qquad
(1\leq t\leq L),
\qquad
a_0:L\to 0.
\]
For each \(\ell\in \{1,\dots,L\}\), let
\[
p_\ell:=a_{\ell-1}\cdots a_1a_0a_L\cdots a_{\ell+1},
\]
the unique length-\(L\) path omitting the arrow \(a_\ell\). Define
\[
I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}:=\langle p_1,\dots,p_L\rangle\subseteq kQ,
\qquad
A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}:=kQ/I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
The only length-\(L\) path not killed by \(I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\) is
\[
p_0:=a_L\cdots a_1.
\]
In the DLN dictionary this is exactly the open-chain product \(\mu(W)\), so it
must survive in the direct weight-space formalism.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Representation varieties]
Fix vector spaces \(V_i\cong k^{d_i}\). Set
\[
\Rep_d(Q)
:=
\prod_{\ell=1}^L \Hom(V_{\ell-1},V_\ell)\times \Hom(V_L,V_0),
\]
and
\[
\Rep_d(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}})
:=
\{\phi\in \Rep_d(Q)\mid p_\ell(\phi)=0\ \text{for all }1\leq \ell\leq L\}.
\]
For a fixed map \(W_0\in \Hom(V_L,V_0)\), define the fixed-arrow slice
\[
\Rep_{d,W_0}(Q,I_L^{\mathrm{wt}})
:=
\{\phi\in \Rep_d(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}})\mid \phi_0=W_0\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[The cyclic locus is a fixed-arrow representation variety]
\label{prop:gamma-slice}
Specialize to \(k=\R\) and fix
\[
W_0=\Sigma_{XY}P_Q.
\]
For \(W=(W_1,\dots,W_L)\in \Omega\), define
\[
\phi(W):=(W_0,W_1,\dots,W_L)\in \Rep_d(Q).
\]
Then
\[
W\in \Gamma_{d,S}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\phi(W)\in \Rep_{d,W_0}(Q,I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For each \(1\leq \ell\leq L\),
\[
p_\ell(\phi(W))
=
W_{\ell-1}\cdots W_1W_0W_L\cdots W_{\ell+1}
=
W_{<\ell}W_0W_{>\ell}.
\]
Thus the quiver relations are exactly the weight-space cyclic equations.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The weight-space algebra}

\begin{lemma}[Surviving paths]
\label{lem:surviving-paths}
In \(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\), the nonzero oriented paths are precisely the path
classes represented by
\[
(a,b)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}
:=
\{(a,b)\in \Z^2\mid 0\leq a\leq L,\ a\leq b\leq a+L\}
\setminus
\{(a,a+L)\mid 1\leq a\leq L\}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Every oriented path on the cycle lifts uniquely to an interval \([a,b]\) on the
universal cover with \(0\leq a\leq L\) and \(a\leq b\leq a+L\). The relation
ideal kills exactly the length-\(L\) paths \((a,a+L)\) with \(1\leq a\leq L\),
and all longer paths contain such a subpath. The unique surviving length-\(L\)
path is \((0,L)\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Projective indecomposables]
\label{cor:projectives}
The indecomposable projectives are
\[
P(0)=M(0,L),
\qquad
P(a)=M(a,a+L-1)\quad (1\leq a\leq L).
\]
Equivalently, the Kupisch series of \(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\) is
\[
(L+1,L,\dots,L).
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:surviving-paths}, the maximal nonzero path starting at \(0\)
has length \(L\), while the maximal nonzero path starting at \(a>0\) has length
\(L-1\).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[The weight-space algebra is Nakayama]
\label{thm:nakayama}
The algebra \(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\) is a finite-dimensional cyclic monomial
Nakayama algebra.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The ideal \(I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\) is monomial, every path of length \(L+1\) is
zero by Lemma~\ref{lem:surviving-paths}, and the quiver is an oriented cycle.
Hence the quotient is finite-dimensional, monomial, and Nakayama
\cite[Chapter~VI]{assem2006}.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Interval modules]
For \((a,b)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\), let \(M(a,b)\) be the interval
representation supported on the lifted interval \([a,b]\): it is one-dimensional
on the vertices visited by \([a,b]\), every arrow inside the support acts by the
identity, and every arrow outside the support acts by zero.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Classification of indecomposables]
\label{thm:indecomp}
The finite-dimensional indecomposable \(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\)-modules are exactly
the interval modules
\[
M(a,b)
\qquad
((a,b)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}),
\]
and each indecomposable occurs uniquely in this form.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:nakayama}, every indecomposable is a quotient of a unique
indecomposable projective. Using Corollary~\ref{cor:projectives}, these
quotients are precisely the interval modules \(M(a,b)\) indexed by the admissible
lifted intervals.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Multiplicity patterns and orbit classification}

\begin{definition}[Multiplicity patterns]
Define
\[
\mathcal M_d^+(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}})
\]
to be the set of arrays
\[
m=(m_{ab})_{(a,b)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}}\in
\Z_{\geq 0}^{\mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}}
\]
such that the module
\[
\bigoplus_{(a,b)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}} M(a,b)^{\oplus m_{ab}}
\]
has dimension vector \(d\).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Krull--Schmidt decomposition]
\label{prop:krull-schmidt}
Every finite-dimensional \(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\)-module of dimension vector \(d\)
decomposes uniquely as
\[
M\cong \bigoplus_{(a,b)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}}
M(a,b)^{\oplus m_{ab}}
\]
for a unique multiplicity pattern
\[
m=(m_{ab})\in \mathcal M_d^+(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The algebra is finite-dimensional, hence artinian, so its module category is
Krull--Schmidt. The indecomposable classification of
Theorem~\ref{thm:indecomp} yields the stated decomposition.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Ambient and fixed-arrow orbit classification]
\label{prop:multiplicity-orbits}
Two points of \(\Rep_d(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}})\) lie in the same \(G_d\)-orbit if and
only if they have the same multiplicity pattern. Two points of
\(\Rep_{d,W_0}(Q,I_L^{\mathrm{wt}})\) lie in the same \(G_d^{W_0}\)-orbit if and
only if they have the same multiplicity pattern.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In both cases, belonging to the same orbit is equivalent to being isomorphic as
\(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\)-modules. By Proposition~\ref{prop:krull-schmidt}, this is
equivalent to equality of multiplicity patterns.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[The module attached to a cyclic point]
For \(W\in \Gamma_{d,S}\), let
\[
M(W)
\]
be the \(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\)-module corresponding to \(\phi(W)\) under
Proposition~\ref{prop:gamma-slice}, and let
\[
m_W=(m_{ab}(W))\in \mathcal M_d^+(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}})
\]
be its multiplicity pattern.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Closing-arrow rank functional]
For \(m\in \mathcal M_d^+(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}})\), define
\[
\nu_0(m)
:=
\sum_{\substack{(a,b)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\\ a\leq L < L+1\leq b}}
m_{ab}.
\]
Equivalently, \(\nu_0(m)\) is the total multiplicity of the indecomposable
intervals whose support contains the closing arrow.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Realized multiplicity patterns]
\label{prop:realized-m}
The realized multiplicity patterns in the fixed-arrow cyclic locus are exactly
those satisfying
\[
\nu_0(m)=\rk(W_0)=d_L-r.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
On an indecomposable interval \(M(a,b)\), the closing arrow \(a_0\) acts with
rank \(1\) if and only if the interval contains the lifted segment
\([L,L+1]\), and otherwise it acts with rank \(0\). Therefore the rank of the
closing-arrow map on the direct sum defined by \(m\) is exactly \(\nu_0(m)\).

If \(m=m_W\) is realized by a point \(W\in \Gamma_{d,S}\), then the closing
arrow is exactly \(W_0\), so necessarily \(\nu_0(m)=\rk(W_0)\).

Conversely, if \(\nu_0(m)=\rk(W_0)\), then the closing-arrow map of the module
defined by \(m\) has the same rank as \(W_0\). Since two real matrices are
equivalent under \(\GL(d_0)\times \GL(d_L)\) if and only if they have the same
rank, an endpoint base change carries the closing arrow to \(W_0\). Transporting
the remaining arrows by the same base change yields a representative in the
fixed-arrow slice.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Multiplicity-pattern stratification of the cyclic locus]
\label{thm:multiplicity-stratification}
The assignment
\[
W\longmapsto m_W
\]
induces a bijection
\[
G_d^{W_0}\backslash \Gamma_{d,S}
\xrightarrow{\sim}
\{m\in \mathcal M_d^+(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}})\mid \nu_0(m)=d_L-r\}.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
\Gamma_{d,S}
=
\bigsqcup_{\substack{m\in \mathcal M_d^+(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}})\\ \nu_0(m)=d_L-r}}
\Gamma_{d,S}[m],
\qquad
\Gamma_{d,S}[m]:=\{W\in \Gamma_{d,S}\mid m_W=m\},
\]
and each nonempty stratum \(\Gamma_{d,S}[m]\) is a single \(G_d^{W_0}\)-orbit.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Combine Proposition~\ref{prop:multiplicity-orbits} with
Proposition~\ref{prop:realized-m}.
\end{proof}

\section{Rank Patterns and Cyclic Rank Stratification}

\subsection{Lifted cyclic rank patterns}

\begin{definition}[Lifted path coordinates]
For \((a,b)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\), let
\[
q_{ab}
\]
denote the unique nonzero oriented path of \(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\) represented by
the lifted interval \([a,b]\).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Lifted cyclic rank pattern]
Let \(M\) be an \(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\)-module of dimension vector \(d\). Its
lifted cyclic rank pattern is the array
\[
\rho(M)=(\rho_{ab}(M))_{(a,b)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}},
\qquad
\rho_{ab}(M):=\rk\bigl(q_{ab}(M)\bigr).
\]
\end{definition}

Two coordinates play a distinguished role:
\[
\rho_{0L}(M)=\rk(q_{0L}(M)),
\qquad
\rho_{L,L+1}(M)=\rk(q_{L,L+1}(M)).
\]
In the DLN dictionary these are exactly the end-to-end rank and the rank of the
closing arrow.

\begin{proposition}[Forward formula]
\label{prop:forward}
Let \(M\) have multiplicity pattern
\[
m=(m_{uv})_{(u,v)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}}.
\]
Then for every \((a,b)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\),
\[
\rho_{ab}(M)
=
\sum_{\substack{(u,v)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\\ u\leq a\leq b\leq v}}
m_{uv}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
On an indecomposable interval module \(M(u,v)\), the path \(q_{ab}\) acts with
rank \(1\) exactly when the path interval \([a,b]\) is contained in \([u,v]\),
and otherwise with rank \(0\). The claim follows by additivity of rank on
direct sums.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Inversion formula]
\label{prop:inversion}
Extend every rank pattern by the convention
\[
\rho_{uv}=0
\qquad
\text{for }(u,v)\notin \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}.
\]
Then for every admissible interval \((a,b)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\),
\[
m_{ab}
=
\rho_{ab}-\rho_{a-1,b}-\rho_{a,b+1}+\rho_{a-1,b+1}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Insert the forward formula into the right-hand side and note that, for every
fixed interval \((u,v)\), the coefficient of \(m_{uv}\) is
\[
\mathbf 1_{u\leq a\leq b\leq v}
-\mathbf 1_{u\leq a-1\leq b\leq v}
-\mathbf 1_{u\leq a\leq b+1\leq v}
+\mathbf 1_{u\leq a-1\leq b+1\leq v}.
\]
This coefficient equals \(1\) precisely when \((u,v)=(a,b)\) and equals \(0\)
otherwise. A more detailed telescoping proof is recorded in
Appendix~\ref{app:inversion}.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Admissible rank-pattern set]
\[
\mathcal R_d^{\mathrm{wt}}
:=
\left\{
\rho\in \Z_{\geq 0}^{\mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}}
\ \middle|\
\begin{array}{l}
\rho_{aa}=d_a \text{ for every }0\leq a\leq L,\\[2pt]
\rho_{ab}-\rho_{a-1,b}-\rho_{a,b+1}+\rho_{a-1,b+1}\geq 0\\
\hspace{2.7cm}\text{for every }(a,b)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}
\end{array}
\right\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Multiplicity-rank bijection]
\label{thm:rank-bijection}
The multiplicity-pattern set and the rank-pattern set are canonically in
bijection:
\[
\mathcal M_d^+(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}})
\xrightarrow{\sim}
\mathcal R_d^{\mathrm{wt}}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The forward map sends a multiplicity pattern to the rank pattern given by
Proposition~\ref{prop:forward}. The inverse map is the finite-difference formula
of Proposition~\ref{prop:inversion}. The diagonal condition \(\rho_{aa}=d_a\)
ensures that the resulting multiplicity pattern has dimension vector \(d\).
\end{proof}

\subsection{Fixed-arrow compatibility in rank coordinates}

\begin{proposition}[Fixed-arrow and rank-\(r\) constraints]
\label{prop:rank-constraints}
Let \(W\in \Gamma_{d,S}\), and let \(\rho(W)\) be its lifted cyclic rank
pattern. Then
\[
\rho_{L,L+1}(W)=\rk(W_0)=d_L-r.
\]
If moreover \(W\in \Gamma_{d,S}^r\), then
\[
\rho_{0L}(W)=\rk(\mu(W))=r.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The first identity is immediate from
\[
q_{L,L+1}(W)=W_0,
\]
and the second from
\[
q_{0L}(W)=W_L\cdots W_1=\mu(W).
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Admissible rank-\(r\) label set]
Define
\[
\mathcal R_{d,S}^{\,r}
:=
\{\rho\in \mathcal R_d^{\mathrm{wt}}\mid \rho_{L,L+1}=d_L-r,\ \rho_{0L}=r\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Rank-pattern stratification of the cyclic locus]
\label{thm:rank-strat-gamma}
The assignment
\[
W\longmapsto \rho(W)
\]
induces bijections
\[
G_d^{W_0}\backslash \Gamma_{d,S}
\xrightarrow{\sim}
\{\rho\in \mathcal R_d^{\mathrm{wt}}\mid \rho_{L,L+1}=d_L-r\},
\]
and
\[
G_d^{W_0}\backslash \Gamma_{d,S}^r
\xrightarrow{\sim}
\mathcal R_{d,S}^{\,r}.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
\Gamma_{d,S}^r
=
\bigsqcup_{\rho\in \mathcal R_{d,S}^{\,r}}
\Gamma_{d,S}^r[\rho],
\qquad
\Gamma_{d,S}^r[\rho]:=\{W\in \Gamma_{d,S}^r\mid \rho(W)=\rho\},
\]
and every nonempty stratum is a single \(G_d^{W_0}\)-orbit.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Compose the multiplicity-pattern stratification theorem
(Theorem~\ref{thm:multiplicity-stratification}) with the bijection of
Theorem~\ref{thm:rank-bijection} and the scalar constraints of
Proposition~\ref{prop:rank-constraints}.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Depth \(L=3\)]
For \(L=3\), the lifted admissible intervals are
\[
(0,0),(0,1),(0,2),(0,3),(1,1),(1,2),(1,3),(2,2),(2,3),(2,4),(3,3),(3,4),(3,5).
\]
The wrapped intervals are \((2,4)\), \((3,4)\), and \((3,5)\), corresponding to
the indecomposables containing the closing arrow. The two distinguished scalar
coordinates are
\[
\rho_{03}=\rk(W_3W_2W_1),
\qquad
\rho_{34}=\rk(W_0).
\]
If \(r=d_3\), then \(\rho_{34}=0\), so the wrapped intervals disappear and the
admissible rank patterns reduce to the usual chain rank patterns for
\[
0\to 1\to 2\to 3.
\]
\end{example}

\section{Stratification of the Critical Loci}

The orbit correspondence of Section~3 now transfers the cyclic rank-pattern
labels to the critical locus.

\begin{definition}[Critical rank-pattern strata]
For \(\rho\in \mathcal R_{d,S}^{\,r}\), define
\[
\mathcal C_{d,S}^r[\rho]
:=
\mathcal C_{d,S}^r\cap \Gamma_{d,S}^r[\rho].
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Main theorem: critical loci are stratified by cyclic rank patterns]
\label{thm:main}
The rank-\(r\) critical locus admits the disjoint decomposition
\[
\mathcal C_{d,S}^r
=
\bigsqcup_{\rho\in \mathcal R_{d,S}^{\,r}}
\mathcal C_{d,S}^r[\rho].
\]
Each nonempty stratum \(\mathcal C_{d,S}^r[\rho]\) is a single \(H_S\)-orbit.
Equivalently, the assignment
\[
W\longmapsto \rho(W)
\]
induces a bijection
\[
H_S\backslash \mathcal C_{d,S}^r
\xrightarrow{\sim}
\mathcal R_{d,S}^{\,r}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:rank-strat-gamma}, every nonempty stratum
\(\Gamma_{d,S}^r[\rho]\) is a single \(G_d^{W_0}\)-orbit. By the orbit
correspondence theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:orbit-bijection}), each such orbit
meets \(\mathcal C_{d,S}^r\) in exactly one \(H_S\)-orbit. Intersecting the
cyclic decomposition with the critical locus yields the stated partition.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Full-rank recovery]
\label{cor:full-rank}
If \(r=d_L\), then
\[
W_0=0,
\qquad
G_d^{W_0}=G_d,
\qquad
\Gamma_{d,S}^{d_L}=\Sigma_d^{d_L}.
\]
In this case the admissible cyclic rank patterns are exactly the chain rank
patterns of the equioriented quiver
\[
0\to 1\to \cdots \to L,
\]
and Theorem~\ref{thm:main} recovers the orbit classification of the global-minimum
fiber.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
If \(r=d_L\), then \(P_Q=0\), so the closing-arrow rank constraint is
\[
\rho_{L,L+1}=0.
\]
Hence no wrapped interval occurs, and only the chain intervals remain. The orbit
correspondence then becomes
\[
\mathcal C_{d,S}^{d_L}/H_S \xrightarrow{\sim} \Sigma_d^{d_L}/G_d.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Combinatorics of saddle strata]
Theorem~\ref{thm:main} gives a discrete identity to each saddle stratum. Deep
linear networks do not have isolated generic saddle points; instead, each saddle
class is an \(H_S\)-orbit inside a positive-dimensional critical locus. The
cyclic rank pattern is the finite combinatorial label that distinguishes these
orbit classes.
\end{remark}

\section{Codimension Formulas}

\subsection{Hom spaces and the endomorphism functional}

\begin{proposition}[Hom criterion for interval modules]
\label{prop:hom-criterion}
For admissible intervals \((a,b),(c,d)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\),
\[
\dim_\R \Hom_A(M(a,b),M(c,d))
=
\begin{cases}
1, & [a,b]\subseteq [c,d],\\
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Every interval module is one-dimensional on its support and all internal arrow
maps are identities. A morphism is therefore determined by a single scalar on
the source support and exists exactly when the source support is contained in the
target support.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Endomorphism functional]
For
\[
m=(m_{ab})\in \mathcal M_d^+(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}),
\]
define
\[
\mathsf E(m)
:=
\sum_{(a,b)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}}
\sum_{(c,d)\in \mathcal I_L^{\mathrm{wt}}}
m_{ab}m_{cd}\,\mathbf 1_{[a,b]\subseteq [c,d]}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Endomorphism dimension]
\label{prop:end-dim}
If a module \(M\) has multiplicity pattern \(m\), then
\[
\dim_\R \End_A(M)=\mathsf E(m).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By additivity of \(\Hom\) on finite direct sums and
Proposition~\ref{prop:hom-criterion},
\[
\dim \End_A(M)
=
\sum_{(a,b),(c,d)}
m_{ab}m_{cd}\dim \Hom_A(M(a,b),M(c,d)),
\]
which is exactly \(\mathsf E(m)\).
\end{proof}

\subsection{Dimensions of the acting groups}

\begin{proposition}[Dimension of \(G_d^{W_0}\)]
\label{prop:dim-GW0}
Let
\[
s:=\rk(W_0)=d_L-r.
\]
Then
\[
\dim G_d^{W_0}
=
\sum_{\ell=0}^L d_\ell^2-s(d_0+d_L-s)
=
\sum_{\ell=0}^L d_\ell^2-(d_L-r)(d_0+r).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The hidden-layer factors contribute \(\sum_{\ell=1}^{L-1} d_\ell^2\). The
endpoint factor is the stabilizer of a rank-\(s\) matrix under the left-right
action of \(\GL(d_0)\times \GL(d_L)\), hence has dimension
\[
d_0^2+d_L^2-s(d_0+d_L-s).
\]
Adding the contributions gives the result.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Dimension of \(H_S\)]
\label{prop:dim-HS}
One has
\[
\dim H_S=\sum_{\ell=0}^L d_\ell^2-d_0d_L.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Set
\[
U:=\Ker(W_0),
\qquad
I:=\im(W_0).
\]
Then
\[
\dim U=r,
\qquad
\dim(\R^{d_0}/I)=d_0-d_L+r.
\]
By the normalization theorem, the quotient \(K_S/H_S\) is the left-right orbit
of the reduced distinguished product
\[
\overline T:\R^{d_0}/I\to U,
\]
which is surjective of rank \(r\). The orbit of a rank-\(r\) map between spaces
of dimensions \(d_0-d_L+r\) and \(r\) has dimension
\[
r(d_0-d_L+r).
\]
Thus
\[
\dim H_S
=
\dim G_d^{W_0}-r(d_0-d_L+r).
\]
Substituting Proposition~\ref{prop:dim-GW0} and simplifying yields
\[
\dim H_S=\sum_{\ell=0}^L d_\ell^2-d_0d_L.
\]
\end{proof}

\subsection{Stratum codimension formulas}

\begin{definition}[Ambient dimension]
We write
\[
\dim \Omega=\sum_{\ell=1}^L d_\ell d_{\ell-1}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Codimension of the cyclic and critical strata]
\label{thm:codim-strata}
Let \(m\in \mathcal M_d^+(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}})\) be a multiplicity pattern realized
in \(\Gamma_{d,S}^r\). Then
\[
\codim_{\Omega}\Gamma_{d,S}^r[m]
=
\Bigl(
\sum_{\ell=1}^L d_\ell d_{\ell-1}
-\sum_{\ell=0}^L d_\ell^2
+(d_L-r)(d_0+r)
\Bigr)
+
\mathsf E(m),
\]
and
\[
\codim_{\Omega}\mathcal C_{d,S}^r[m]
=
\Bigl(
\sum_{\ell=1}^L d_\ell d_{\ell-1}
-\sum_{\ell=0}^L d_\ell^2
+d_0d_L
\Bigr)
+
\mathsf E(m).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(W\in \Gamma_{d,S}^r[m]\). The stabilizer of \(W\) in \(G_d^{W_0}\) is
\(\Aut_A(M(W))\), the module automorphism group of the corresponding
\(A_L^{\mathrm{wt}}\)-module. This is an open subset of the affine space
\(\End_A(M(W))\), hence has the same dimension:
\[
\dim \Aut_A(M(W))=\dim \End_A(M(W))=\mathsf E(m)
\]
by Proposition~\ref{prop:end-dim}. Therefore
\[
\dim \Gamma_{d,S}^r[m]
=
\dim(G_d^{W_0}\cdot W)
=
\dim G_d^{W_0}-\mathsf E(m).
\]
Subtracting from \(\dim \Omega\) and using Proposition~\ref{prop:dim-GW0} gives
the cyclic formula.

Now let \(W_c\in \mathcal C_{d,S}^r[m]\). By Theorem~\ref{thm:main},
\(\mathcal C_{d,S}^r[m]\) is a single \(H_S\)-orbit. Its stabilizer in \(H_S\)
is again \(\Aut_A(M(W_c))\), so
\[
\dim \mathcal C_{d,S}^r[m]
=
\dim H_S-\mathsf E(m).
\]
Subtracting from \(\dim \Omega\) and using Proposition~\ref{prop:dim-HS} gives
the critical formula.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Rank-pattern version]
\label{cor:codim-rank}
For \(\rho\in \mathcal R_{d,S}^{\,r}\), define
\[
m_{ab}(\rho):=\rho_{ab}-\rho_{a-1,b}-\rho_{a,b+1}+\rho_{a-1,b+1},
\]
and
\[
\mathsf E(\rho)
:=
\sum_{(a,b),(c,d)}
m_{ab}(\rho)m_{cd}(\rho)\,\mathbf 1_{[a,b]\subseteq [c,d]}.
\]
Then
\[
\codim_{\Omega}\Gamma_{d,S}^r[\rho]
=
\Bigl(
\sum_{\ell=1}^L d_\ell d_{\ell-1}
-\sum_{\ell=0}^L d_\ell^2
+(d_L-r)(d_0+r)
\Bigr)
+
\mathsf E(\rho),
\]
and
\[
\codim_{\Omega}\mathcal C_{d,S}^r[\rho]
=
\Bigl(
\sum_{\ell=1}^L d_\ell d_{\ell-1}
-\sum_{\ell=0}^L d_\ell^2
+d_0d_L
\Bigr)
+
\mathsf E(\rho).
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
This is the multiplicity formula of Theorem~\ref{thm:codim-strata} rewritten
via the inversion formula of Proposition~\ref{prop:inversion}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Whole-locus codimension formulas]
\label{cor:codim-whole}
One has
\[
\codim_{\Omega}\Gamma_{d,S}^r
=
\Bigl(
\sum_{\ell=1}^L d_\ell d_{\ell-1}
-\sum_{\ell=0}^L d_\ell^2
+(d_L-r)(d_0+r)
\Bigr)
+
\min_{\rho\in \mathcal R_{d,S}^{\,r}}\mathsf E(\rho),
\]
and
\[
\codim_{\Omega}\mathcal C_{d,S}^r
=
\Bigl(
\sum_{\ell=1}^L d_\ell d_{\ell-1}
-\sum_{\ell=0}^L d_\ell^2
+d_0d_L
\Bigr)
+
\min_{\rho\in \mathcal R_{d,S}^{\,r}}\mathsf E(\rho).
\]
Equivalently,
\[
\codim_{\Omega}\mathcal C_{d,S}^r
=
\codim_{\Omega}\Gamma_{d,S}^r+r(d_0-d_L+r).
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Each locus is a finite union of orbits, so its dimension is the maximum of the
dimensions of its strata. Equivalently, its codimension is the minimum of the
codimensions of its strata. Apply Corollary~\ref{cor:codim-rank} and subtract
the two constants.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Full-rank case]
\label{cor:full-rank-codim}
If \(r=d_L\), then
\[
\Gamma_{d,S}^{d_L}=\Sigma_d^{d_L}
\]
is open in \(\Omega\), hence
\[
\codim_{\Omega}\mathcal C_{d,S}^{d_L}=d_0d_L.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The full-rank determinantal locus is open under the no-bottleneck assumption, so
\[
\codim_{\Omega}\Gamma_{d,S}^{d_L}=0.
\]
Now apply Corollary~\ref{cor:codim-whole}.
\end{proof}

\section{Discussion and Future Work}

The critical loci of deep linear networks are not discrete, so the usual
language of isolated saddles is not appropriate. Theorems~\ref{thm:rank-strat-gamma}
and~\ref{thm:main} provide the missing discrete structure: every critical orbit
of rank \(r\) carries a unique cyclic rank pattern. This is the combinatorial
identity of a saddle stratum.

The direct weight-space formulation is important here. It shows that the cyclic
equations, the critical condition, and the relevant orbit problems all live in
the same parameter space \(\Omega\). The cyclic locus is not just an auxiliary
construction; it is the ambient space whose orbit combinatorics transfers
directly to the critical locus.

The codimension formulas of Section~7 suggest several further directions.
First, the function \(\mathsf E(\rho)\) gives a finite optimization problem for
the codimension of the critical locus, and it is natural to ask for a more
compact closed form. Second, the partial order of orbit closures in
\(\mathcal R_{d,S}^{\,r}\) should encode how saddle strata degenerate into one
another. Third, the codimension shift
\[
r(d_0-d_L+r)
\]
between the cyclic and critical loci is the first indication of an
``RLCT-type'' contribution associated with saddles. Unlike the real log
canonical threshold at a minimum, no standard invariant is currently available
for a saddle locus, but the present stratification suggests a natural
combinatorial proxy.

Finally, the practical motivation is dynamical. In deep linear networks, the
training trajectory of stochastic gradient descent interacts with critical loci
rather than isolated points. A finite combinatorial model of saddle strata is a
first step toward understanding how these loci shape optimization trajectories.

\section{Proof of the Inversion Formula}
\label{app:inversion}

We give a slightly more explicit version of the proof of
Proposition~\ref{prop:inversion}. For every admissible interval \((a,b)\), the
forward formula gives
\[
\rho_{ab}
=
\sum_{u\leq a\leq b\leq v} m_{uv}.
\]
Therefore
\[
\rho_{ab}-\rho_{a-1,b}-\rho_{a,b+1}+\rho_{a-1,b+1}
=
\sum_{u,v} m_{uv}\,\Xi_{uv},
\]
where
\[
\Xi_{uv}
:=
\mathbf 1_{u\leq a\leq b\leq v}
-\mathbf 1_{u\leq a-1\leq b\leq v}
-\mathbf 1_{u\leq a\leq b+1\leq v}
+\mathbf 1_{u\leq a-1\leq b+1\leq v}.
\]

If \(u<a\), then the first two indicators cancel, and likewise the last two
indicators cancel. If \(v>b\), then the first and third indicators cancel, and
likewise the second and fourth indicators cancel. The only way for a nonzero
coefficient to remain is therefore
\[
u=a,\qquad v=b.
\]
In that case
\[
\Xi_{ab}=1.
\]
Thus the entire sum reduces to \(m_{ab}\), proving the inversion formula.

\section{Projectives, Syzygies, and the \texorpdfstring{\(\Ext^1\)}{Ext1} Criterion}
\label{app:ext}

This appendix records the homological comparison with the older codimension
approach.

\begin{proposition}[First syzygy of an interval module]
Let \(M(a,b)\) be a nonprojective interval module. Define
\[
\beta(a):=
\begin{cases}
L, & a=0,\\
a+L-1, & 1\leq a\leq L.
\end{cases}
\]
Then there is a short exact sequence
\[
0\to M(b+1,\beta(a))\to P(a)\to M(a,b)\to 0.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By Corollary~\ref{cor:projectives}, the projective cover of \(M(a,b)\) is
\[
P(a)=M(a,\beta(a)).
\]
The kernel is the tail interval remaining after quotienting by the first
\(b-a+1\) composition factors, namely \(M(b+1,\beta(a))\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[\(\Ext^1\)-criterion]
For admissible intervals \((a,b)\) and \((c,d)\),
\[
\dim_\R \Ext_A^1(M(a,b),M(c,d))
=
\begin{cases}
1, & M(a,b)\ \text{nonprojective and } [b+1,\beta(a)]\subseteq [c,d],\\
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply \(\Hom_A(-,M(c,d))\) to the short exact sequence above and use
Proposition~\ref{prop:hom-criterion}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Comparison with the old codimension strategy]
The older residual-space approach expressed codimensions by deformation theory
and \(\Ext^1\)-spaces. In the present paper the orbit decomposition is already
exact, so the codimension formulas of Section~7 are obtained more directly from
group dimensions and endomorphism spaces. The \(\Ext^1\)-criterion remains
useful as a consistency check, but it is no longer needed for the main theorem.
\end{remark}
